01 Showmax Mobile streaming · 2020–2024

Designed a better mobile streaming experience for viewers where data is expensive, networks are unstable, and power cuts are routine.

Showmax serves customers across Africa, where streaming is shaped by very different constraints than in most Western markets. In many households, Android was the main or only device, and offline access, efficient playback, and fast content discovery had a direct impact on retention and viewing time.

Role

Product Designer, May 2020 – March 2024. Sole designer on the product, owning the mobile experience across Android and iOS in a continuous discovery setup.

Focus

Two customer interviews per week for two years, quarterly outcomes tied to retention, content discovery, and time spent streaming.

How I worked

Ran two customer interviews per week with my PM for more than two years. Designed the in-app recruitment form, structured the research repository, and worked from quarterly outcomes tied to retention, fast content discovery, and time spent streaming.

Used an Opportunity Solution Tree to connect user problems to testable assumptions and prioritise work based on evidence and shipping effort.

What we were solving

The top three problems were power cuts, network instability, and content discovery. Keeping people engaged during outages and always giving them something new to watch was not optional — it had a direct effect on revenue.

One assumption we spent time on was that customers found it hard to identify content that felt new on Showmax. That shaped how we thought about discovery.

App player

The player was crowded with unlabeled, technical controls most users did not trust or understand. That made the interface feel intimidating and left no real estate for the controls that actually mattered during playback.

We studied usage data and screen recordings to understand which features were used, which were ignored, and where users got stuck. That led to several concrete decisions: moving low-use features under an Advanced menu, keeping audio, subtitles, and video quality visible and clearly labelled, repositioning the timeline to reduce accidental system-gesture conflicts, and introducing gestures for playback, brightness, and volume.

I also introduced screen lock after repeated complaints in interviews from parents and grandparents whose kids kept interrupting playback. It became one of the most warmly received changes in app store reviews.

Downloads

Downloads were underused, even though they were one of the best tools for getting through power cuts and saving on data.

We found that large groups of users did not know the feature existed or did not understand what it was for. I redesigned the empty state into a three-step onboarding flow that explained the feature and nudged users toward finding something to download. I also cleaned up the UI, moved management actions into context sheets and separate screens, replaced technical file-size language with plain terms, and grouped TV episodes into folders by series and season.

Impact

of playbacks used lower video quality to save data

~35%

of users interacted with the episode list

55%

of playbacks used the new gestures regularly

+10%

completed downloads in the following weeks

+4%

All metrics are hard numbers from month-to-month tracking across A/B tests and production rollouts.

Systems work

Built the mobile design system library collaboratively from scratch across squads and platforms, following platform-specific patterns for Android and iOS rather than forcing a single approach onto both. The library sped up prototyping, maintained consistency across the app, and made Design QA sharper and faster.

What I take from it

This was the role where I understood what continuous product improvement actually means in practice: not big launches, but a reliable system for learning, deciding, and shipping something better every few weeks. Working under real user constraints made every design decision feel consequential.
02 Direct Fidoo Fintech marketplace · Pre-MVP → launch · 2025–2026

Designed one setup experience for a marketplace that had to work across seven partners and four different integration patterns.

Direct Fidoo is a neo-bank for SMEs in Central Europe, built as a fintech branch of Direct Group. The Ecosystems area gave customers access to third-party financial services — insurance, credit loans, and factoring — intended to help their businesses grow. Each partner operated at a different technical level, from pen-and-paper lead generation to full API integration.

Role

Product Designer for the Ecosystems and Marketplace experience. Worked with a product owner to design and deliver the end-to-end marketplace, pre-MVP through launch.

Outcome

Launched with seven partners and four integration patterns, all handled by a single setup wizard, alongside the design system I built with the product.

The problem

Customers entered the Marketplace and saw a catalog of third-party services: factoring, business insurance, fleet insurance, credit loans, and more — presented as product cards. The intended action was to find a relevant service and set it up.

The underlying reality was more complex. Each partner required different data, used different technology, and operated at a different level of integration. Without a consistent setup structure, every new partner would have produced a different experience, and customers would have had no reliable frame for what to expect.

What I built

I designed a wizard that served as the single entry point for all seven partners, regardless of integration depth.

The opening steps were the same for everyone: select the company the service was intended for, select the contact person, and review a short partner overview with three key benefits. From there, the wizard adapted — collecting the specific data each partner needed, such as the highest-revenue business entity, key customer details, or PSD2 financial consent.

The final step depended on the integration level: lead generation ended with review and confirmation; intermediate integrations redirected to a partner wizard or displayed a customised iframe; full API integrations were built step by step, with business insurance as the most complex flow.

The design covered four distinct integration patterns. Where possible, I worked with partners to align their frontend to our experience. Where that was not possible, I pushed for an explicit redirect rather than a seam that would confuse customers.

Pushing back

The most significant pushback came when a factoring partner wanted to use an API for looking up statutory bodies in public registers, with the business proposing to trigger that onboarding in the middle of a factoring application.

I flagged it as contextually wrong: the customer had started a specific task, and interrupting them with an unrelated onboarding flow they had never asked for would hurt conversion for both Fidoo and the partner. A follow-up meeting with the partner ended with the idea being dropped entirely.

Discovery under constraint

The product was pre-MVP when I joined, which meant there were no real customers to test with yet. Research operations were unable to recruit the right respondents — this was a B2B product, and we needed CFOs, CEOs, and procurement decision-makers, not general users.

My PO and I worked around it. I recruited qualified respondents from my professional network and designed a recruitment poster for a nearby coworking space, targeting local businesses frequented by decision-makers. The sample was small, but the people were right.

What shipped

The Marketplace launched with seven partners and four integration patterns — all handled by the same wizard. The launch scope matched the design system I had built alongside the product. After two waves of layoffs reduced the product and engineering team significantly, the decision was made to ship what was complete rather than continue building.

What I take from it

The most valuable thing a designer can do in an early-stage product is make the complex manageable without pretending it is simple. At Fidoo, that meant designing for variability, keeping customers oriented across integration types they would never see, and being willing to say no to features that were technically possible but user-experience wrong.
